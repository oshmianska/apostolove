Apostolove and the Voices of the Steppe

The tale you have just read comes from Apostolove, a small town in southern Ukraine. Today it appears on maps as one of many settlements of the Dnipro region, but for me it is much more than a geographical location. It is a place of family memory.
Apostolove lies in the wide Ukrainian steppe, in a landscape shaped by rivers, winds, fields, and long horizons. For centuries, people here lived close to the rhythms of nature. Harvests, droughts, storms, and changing seasons were not distant concepts but realities that influenced everyday life. In such places, stories were not merely entertainment. They were a way of preserving knowledge, values, fears, and hopes.
The tales presented here were passed down through my family, told by my great-grandmother and older relatives. Like many folktales, it changed slightly with each retelling. Some details were forgotten, others expanded. Yet its heart remained the same.
When I began collecting and recording these stories, I often thought about how fragile oral traditions can be. A tale may survive for generations and then disappear within a single lifetime if nobody chooses to remember it.
In this respect, Ukraine and Japan share a remarkable similarity. Just as Japanese scholars preserved local traditions before they vanished from rural communities, many Ukrainians have sought to preserve village stories before modernization, political upheaval, and war could erase them. Folktales are not only stories. They are records of how ordinary people understood the world around them.
This book is one small attempt to preserve one such voice from the Ukrainian steppe.
